Breakthroughs in Early Detection

Recent advancements in Alzheimer's research have focused on identifying the disease before symptoms appear, when interventions may be most effective:

Blood tests: Several promising blood tests can now detect amyloid and tau proteins with high accuracy. These minimally invasive tests could soon replace more expensive PET scans or spinal taps for initial screening. Researchers at Washington University developed a test that's 94% accurate at identifying elevated amyloid levels.

Eye scans: Studies show that retinal scans can detect beta-amyloid deposits years before symptoms emerge. The retina, as an extension of the brain, shows similar pathological changes. This technology could enable routine screening during regular eye exams.

Digital biomarkers: Researchers are analyzing speech patterns, typing speed, and even walking gait captured by smartphones and wearables to detect subtle cognitive changes. These passive monitoring tools could provide early warning signs while people go about their daily lives.
